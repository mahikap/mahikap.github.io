  • Dear Reader,

    I am Mahika Phutane, a PhD student in Computer Science at Cornell University.
    I'm ever grateful to be advised by Shiri Azenkot at the Enhancing Ability Lab, where I attempt to answer the questions:

    → how can we better understand, design, and deploy systems for accessibility?
    → what are the social and technical complexities surrounding assistive technologies?
    → can we facilitate interactions that cater to different abilities?

    The answers to these questions are motivated by the fields of human-computer interaction, ubiquitous computing, machine learning, disability studies, and education. My research is supported by the Cornell Computer Science Fellowship, as well as the Dennis Washington Leadership Graduate Fellowship.

    Prior to Cornell, I completed my B.Sc. in Computer Science and CCIT (Communication, Culture, Information and Technology) at the University of Toronto, where I first learned about the world of research. For this, I thank my professors Daniel Zingaro, Jenna Hartel, and Cosmin Munteanu.
    Thank you for reading and I welcome you to read further where I share more about my past, present, and future.

    Take Care,

    Mahika

    • RESEARCH ONGOINGS


    User Experience of Voice Interactions

    The "look and feel" of a website often stems from its visual aesthetic, which are inaccessible to people with vision impairments. I'm investigating how voice agents (sreenreaders, voice assistants) can recover this visual and ambient information. Specifically, how can we creatively design voice interactions to deliver the ambience, trust, and emotional responses that are often generated through visual design?

    SUPERVISED BY: DR. SHIRI AZENKOT, DR. QIAN YANG

    Microgressive Experiences of People with Disabilities

    Social media is a place where marginalized communities are susceptible to subtle forms of discrimination and harm; perpetuating experiences of feeling unwelcomed. Through this study, we aim to understand how people with disabilities experience microaggressions (e.g., ableism) on social media platforms, and how their social media experience is shaped through such disability-specific microaggressions.

    SUPERVISED BY: DR. ADITYA VASHISHTA, DR. MEGH MARATHE
